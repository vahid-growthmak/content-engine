## Final Post

95% of AI pilots fail to show P&L return.

Yours is probably in that 95%.

Here's how to know for sure.

↓

I've watched smart founders go through the same cycle more times than I can count.

Big announcement: "We're going AI-first."
Big budget: Six figures on tools, consultants, and internal training.
Big silence: Six months later, when someone asks what it returned.

BCG found that 74% of companies fail to scale their AI pilots beyond the proof-of-concept stage.

MIT research adds a harder number: less than 10% of AI adopters say it has materially moved their P&L.

These aren't small companies making amateur mistakes. These are funded, sophisticated teams — with dedicated AI budgets and boardroom mandates.

So why does it keep failing?

One reason.

They're solving for impressiveness, not impact.

They pick the flashiest use case.
They buy the most-hyped tool.
They run the pilot, produce a deck, declare success.

Nobody asks: "What specific number did this move?"

---

The AI projects that actually work look completely different.

They start boring.

They pick the one workflow that's:
→ Slow and repetitive
→ Directly upstream of revenue
→ Already measured with a baseline number

Then they instrument it. Automate it. Measure before and after.

That's the whole strategy. No transformation roadmap. No AI committee. Just: fix one measurable thing.

---

Here's a 3-question diagnostic. If you can't answer all three clearly, your AI pilot is theatre — not an investment.

**Q1: What is the exact workflow AI is replacing, and what specific number is it supposed to move?**

Not "improve efficiency." A real KPI. CAC. CPL. Lead response time. Close rate. One number.

**Q2: Do you have a pre-automation baseline?**

If you didn't measure it before, you cannot claim the win after. This sounds obvious. Almost nobody does it.

**Q3: Is this workflow directly upstream of revenue — or just convenient to automate?**

Summarizing your Slack threads with AI feels like progress. It doesn't move your pipeline.

---

If your answers were vague — that's not failure. That's just the wrong starting point.

The right question was never "what can AI do?"

It's: "What's the one broken thing, with a real number attached, that AI can fix this quarter?"

Fix that. One thing. This quarter.
