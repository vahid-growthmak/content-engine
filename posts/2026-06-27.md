Unpopular opinion: Most founders adopting AI right now are doing it to look innovative — not to solve a real problem.

The bill comes later.

Here's what the data actually says:

BCG, MIT, and RAND have all studied enterprise AI deployments. The number that keeps showing up? 95% of AI pilots fail to show a measurable P&L return.

Not because AI doesn't work.

Because the problem was chosen wrong.

I've audited dozens of companies running AI tools. The pattern is always the same:

The team demos a shiny new product.
The founder signs the contract.
The workflow gets built around the tool — not around the problem.
6 months later, no one can name a single metric it moved.

That's AI theatre.

It looks productive. It generates reports. It gives your team something to present to the board.

But it doesn't move revenue.

The 5% who get real ROI? They start from a completely different place.

They don't ask: "What AI tool should we adopt?"

They ask: "What is the single most expensive broken workflow in our business right now?"

Then they find the tool that fixes that specific thing.

Before your next AI investment, run these 3 questions:

1. What specific metric is this supposed to move — and by how much, in what timeframe?
2. Do we have the clean data this tool needs to function? Or are we feeding garbage in?
3. If this works perfectly, does it actually touch revenue — or does it just make an internal process feel smoother?

If you can't answer all three, you don't have an AI strategy.

You have a subscription.

The founders building real AI ROI in 2026 aren't the ones with the most tools.

They're the ones who fixed one broken workflow first — and measured it with real numbers.

One workflow. Clean data. Compounding results.

That's the difference between AI that moves your P&L and AI that moves your team into longer meetings.
